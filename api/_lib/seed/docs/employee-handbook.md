# Employee Handbook

Welcome to Northwind. This handbook explains how we work, what we value, and where to go when you need help. It applies to US-based, full-time employees and is meant to be practical rather than exhaustive. When something here doesn't cover your situation, ask your manager or People team — we'd rather you ask than guess.

This is a living document. We update it as the company grows, so check back when policies are announced.

## Our Values

Everything at Northwind ties back to three values. They're not posters on a wall — they show up in how we hire, give feedback, and make decisions.

- **Innovation.** We're here to build things that didn't exist before. We prize good judgment over rigid process, run small experiments before big bets, and treat being wrong as the cost of learning. If a better way exists, propose it.
- **Ownership.** See a problem, own the outcome — not just your slice of it. Owners follow through, communicate proactively when things slip, and don't wait for permission to do the obviously right thing. "That's not my job" is not how we operate.
- **Candor.** We say the true thing kindly and early. Give direct feedback, disagree in the room rather than in the hallway, and once a decision is made, commit to it. Candor is a gift, not an attack — assume good intent on both sides.

## How We Communicate

We're a mostly async company. Default to writing things down so people in different time zones and schedules can stay in sync without being online at the same moment.

- **Slack** is for quick, async conversation. Use public channels over DMs so context is searchable. Don't expect instant replies — if it's urgent, say so and say why. Emoji reactions are a valid, low-noise acknowledgment.
- **Email** is for formal or external communication: offers, legal, vendor conversations, anything you'd want a durable record of. Internal announcements may go by email, but day-to-day work lives in Slack and docs.
- **Docs** are where decisions and designs live. If a Slack thread runs long or a decision matters, write it up and link it.
- **Meetings** are for discussion, alignment, and decisions that genuinely need real-time back-and-forth — not status updates that could be a message.

### Meeting Norms

- Default meeting lengths are **25 or 50 minutes**, not 30 or 60. The gap gives everyone a breather and time to get to the next thing.
- Every meeting has an owner, an agenda, and a reason to be live. No agenda, no meeting.
- Decline meetings you don't need to be in — that's ownership, not rudeness. Ask for notes instead.
- Keep one meeting-light block on the calendar so people have room for deep work.

## Working Hours & Flexibility

Northwind is remote-friendly and outcome-focused. We care about impact and reliability, not hours logged or a seat being warm.

- **Core hours are 10am–4pm local time.** During core hours, be reachable for collaboration, meetings, and quick questions. Outside of them, arrange your day how you work best.
- Work the schedule that lets you do your best work and overlaps enough with your team. If your role or team needs specific coverage, your manager will make that explicit.
- Remote, hybrid, and in-office all work here. Whatever your setup, keep your calendar and Slack status current so people know when to expect you.
- Async-first means you're not expected to answer messages the moment they arrive — and you're not expected to be available at night or on weekends. Protect your off time and respect others'.

## Code of Conduct

We want Northwind to be a place where people do their best work and treat each other well. That takes everyone.

- Treat colleagues, customers, and partners with respect. We don't tolerate harassment, discrimination, bullying, or retaliation of any kind.
- Build an inclusive environment. Different backgrounds and viewpoints make our work better; make room for them.
- Protect confidential and customer data. Follow security policies, use approved tools, and don't share what shouldn't be shared.
- Disclose conflicts of interest — financial, personal, or otherwise — to your manager or People team so we can handle them openly.
- Use good judgment with company resources and your Northwind identity, including on public channels and social media.

If something feels wrong, speak up. Raise concerns with your manager, People, or through our confidential reporting channel. Reports made in good faith will never be held against you.

## Expenses & Travel

Spend company money like it's your own — thoughtfully, and in service of the work.

- **Reasonable, business-related expenses are reimbursable.** Think: tools, software, team meals, professional development, and travel for work.
- Submit expenses through the expense tool with an itemized receipt within **30 days**. Add a short note on the business purpose so approvals are quick.
- Anything unusual or above your team's limit should get a manager's okay **before** you spend.
- For **travel**: book in economy for domestic flights, choose reasonably priced hotels, and prefer refundable or flexible options when plans are uncertain. Book through the approved travel tool when possible.
- When in doubt about whether something is covered, ask Finance first — it's faster than a rejected report later.

## How to Get Help

You're never expected to figure it out alone. Here's who to go to for what:

- **Your manager** — priorities, career growth, blockers, feedback, and anything about your role. Start here for most things.
- **People / HR** — benefits, pay, workplace concerns, conflicts, leaves, accommodations, and anything sensitive.
- **IT / Help Desk** — laptops, accounts, access, software, and Wi-Fi. Post in the IT support channel or file a ticket.
- **Finance** — expenses, reimbursements, travel booking, and vendor payments.
- **Security** — anything that looks like a phishing attempt, data exposure, or lost device. Report it immediately; speed matters more than certainty.
- **Legal** — contracts, external communications, and compliance questions.

Not sure where to start? Ask in the **#help** channel and someone will point you to the right place. Asking questions is a sign of ownership, not weakness — we'd all rather answer one question now than fix a bigger problem later.

Welcome aboard. We're glad you're here.
